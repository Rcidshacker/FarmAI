# 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Pink Hibiscus Mealybug (PHM), *Maconellicoccus hirsutus* (Green), is a highly polyphagous invasive pest native to southern Asia, posing significant threats to agriculture, particularly in tropical and subtropical regions. It has spread globally, affecting over 200 plant genera, with notable economic impacts on crops like custard apple (*Annona squamosa* and *A. reticulata*) in India, including Maharashtra. PHM causes direct damage through sap feeding, leading to chlorosis, deformation, and premature fruit drop, and indirect damage via honeydew secretion promoting sooty mold and secondary infections. In custard apple, infestations primarily target fruits, buds, and young shoots, with peak vulnerability during fruit set and development stages, potentially causing up to 80% yield loss under heavy infestation.

PHM exhibits rapid reproduction, with females laying 150–600 eggs parthenogenetically or sexually, completing up to 15 generations annually in tropical climates. Development is temperature-dependent, optimal at 25–30°C, with lower and upper thresholds around 15°C and 35°C. In India, populations peak during dry summer months (March–June), influenced positively by temperature and negatively by rainfall and humidity. Biocontrol using parasitoids like *Anagyrus kamali* and predators like *Cryptolaemus montrouzieri* has proven effective, reducing populations by 70–95% in field trials. Climate change may expand its range northward in India, increasing voltinism and infestation risks in Maharashtra's custard apple orchards.

### Top 5 Management Recommendations

1. **Biocontrol Integration**: Release *Anagyrus kamali* (parasitoid) and *Cryptolaemus montrouzieri* (predator) at 500–1000/ha during early infestation; achieves 80–95% suppression (Meyerdirk et al., 2001).
2. **Cultural Practices**: Prune infested parts, remove weeds/alternate hosts, and apply sticky bands to trunks to disrupt ant-mealybug mutualism; monitor during fruit set (March–May in Maharashtra).
3. **Chemical Control (Targeted)**: Use profenofos (0.05%) or buprofezin (0.025%) as foliar sprays at economic thresholds; rotate to avoid resistance (Anand & Ayub, 2000).
4. **Monitoring & Forecasting**: Use degree-day models (base 15°C) to predict generations; scout fruits weekly for crawlers; integrate weather data for peak alerts.
5. **Quarantine & Prevention**: Inspect planting material; avoid movement from infested areas; promote resistant custard apple varieties like Arka Sahan.

## SECTION 1: TAXONOMIC & IDENTIFICATION INFORMATION

### 1.1 SCIENTIFIC CLASSIFICATION

*Maconellicoccus hirsutus* (Green, 1908) is a polyphagous mealybug pest. Common names vary regionally: pink hibiscus mealybug (English, global), grape mealybug (India), hibiscus mealybug (Caribbean), pink mealybug (Australia). Taxonomic hierarchy: Kingdom Animalia, Phylum Arthropoda, Subphylum Hexapoda, Class Insecta, Order Hemiptera, Suborder Sternorrhyncha, Family Pseudococcidae, Subfamily Pseudococcinae, Tribe Trabutinini, Genus *Maconellicoccus*, Species *hirsutus*. Related species include *Planococcus citri* (citrus mealybug, distinguished by longer dorsal setae) and *Phenacoccus solenopsis* (cotton mealybug, with transverse wax bands). Historical synonyms: *Phenacoccus hirsutus*, *Phenacoccus glomeratus*, *Maconellicoccus perforatus*. Reclassified based on molecular and morphological studies.

### 1.2 MORPHOLOGICAL IDENTIFICATION

PHM life stages are distinct. Eggs: Orange turning pink, 0.3–0.4 mm, clustered in ovisac of white waxy filaments. Crawler (1st instar): 0.3 mm long, light orange, mobile, no wax. 2nd/3rd instars: Pinkish, developing wax coating, sessile. Adult female: 2–3 mm, reddish-pink, ovoid, wingless, covered in white mealy wax, 9 antennal segments. Adult male: 1.5 mm, slender, winged (one pair), short-lived. Diagnostic: Pink body fluid when crushed, no long caudal filaments (vs. other mealybugs). Differs from scale insects by legs and antennae presence, waxy ovisac. Field ID: White wax masses on plants, pink eggs/crawlers visible when ovisac opened.

## SECTION 2: COMPLETE LIFE CYCLE BIOLOGY

### 2.1 REPRODUCTIVE BIOLOGY

PHM reproduces sexually or parthenogenetically, with regional variation (parthenogenetic in Egypt/Bihar, sexual in West Bengal/Caribbean). Sex ratio: Female-biased (up to 97% at 25°C). Mating: Males short-lived, mate multiple females. Fecundity: 150–600 eggs (avg. 260–300 at 20–27°C, 100 at 30°C). Oviposition: On host twigs/leaves/fruits, in ovisac over 1 week. Ovisac: White, waxy, elastic, containing clusters.
